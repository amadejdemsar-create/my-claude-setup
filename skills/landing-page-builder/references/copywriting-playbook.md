# Conversion Copywriting Playbook

Copy is the conversion engine; the design is the frame around it. This is how a 25-year conversion copywriter thinks. Read it in Phase 2 (and the Strategy section in Phase 1). For complex offers or when the user wants multiple hook variants, hand off to the `copywriting-storytelling` agent with the strategy block below, then curate.

House writing rules apply throughout: no dashes as punctuation (commas, periods, semicolons, or restructure), no dramatic fragment pairs ("Not X. Y.", "Less noise. More signal."), complete sentences, no invented facts.

## Strategy (do this before writing a single line)

You cannot write the right copy until you know these. Pin them down in Phase 1:

1. **Positioning.** What category does this compete in, and why does it win there? "The X for Y" or "unlike A, we B." If you cannot say why it wins, the page has no spine.
2. **Awareness stage** (Eugene Schwartz). This is the single most important lever; it sets how much you explain versus how hard you close:
   - **Unaware** — they don't know they have the problem. Lead by dramatizing the problem/world, not the product.
   - **Problem-aware** — they feel the pain, don't know solutions exist. Lead with the pain, then reveal the category.
   - **Solution-aware** — they know solutions exist, comparing. Lead with your mechanism and why it is better.
   - **Product-aware** — they know your product, not yet convinced. Lead with proof, differentiation, risk reversal.
   - **Most-aware** — ready to buy, need a nudge. Lead with the offer, urgency, the deal. Short page.
   Match the headline and page length to the stage. A long educational page wastes a most-aware buyer; a bare offer loses an unaware visitor.
3. **The one core message.** If the visitor remembers exactly one sentence, what is it?
4. **The primary objection.** The single biggest reason they hesitate. The page must disarm it.
5. **The one action.** What you want them to do. Everything ladders to it.

## The hero (you have ~5 seconds)

The hero is 80% of the result. It answers, instantly: **what is this, who is it for, why should I care, what do I do next.**

- **Headline = one specific promise or outcome**, in the visitor's language, not the product's. Benefit-led, concrete. "Cut hotel response time 63% without hiring" beats "The future of guest communication."
- **Avoid vague cleverness.** A headline that needs the subhead to be understood has failed. Clever is allowed only after clear.
- **Subhead** = how it works / for whom / the proof, in one line. It earns the headline's claim.
- **Write 2 to 3 headline variants** across different angles (outcome, pain-relief, mechanism, identity) and recommend one with a one-line reason.

Hook angles to draw from: the dream outcome, the enemy/pain removed, the unique mechanism ("how"), speed/ease, the identity the visitor wants, a specific surprising number, a bold contrarian claim you can back.

## The page as an argument

A high-converting page is a logical, skimmable argument: **hook → make them feel understood → show the better world → prove it → remove the risk → ask.** Section flow (adapt to awareness stage and offer):

1. **Hero** — the promise + action.
2. **Social proof strip** — borrow credibility immediately (logos, a hard number, a marquee quote).
3. **Problem / stakes** — name the pain in their words; make them feel understood before pitching. (Heavier for less-aware audiences, light or skipped for most-aware.)
4. **Value / how it works** — 3 to 5 **outcomes, not features.** Translate every feature into "which means you…". Each with a concrete proof point.
5. **Deeper proof** — testimonials with name + role, case-study numbers, before/after, results.
6. **Objection handling / FAQ** — the 3 to 5 real hesitations, answered plainly. Put the primary objection first.
7. **Risk reversal** — guarantee, free trial, no card, cancel anytime. Lower the cost of yes.
8. **Final CTA** — restate the promise and the action with conviction.

## Features into benefits

Never list a feature without its consequence. Use the "which means you…" bridge:

- Feature: "AI triages every guest message." → Benefit: "which means your front desk stops drowning in repetitive questions and answers the ones that matter in seconds."

Lead with the benefit; let the feature substantiate it. The visitor buys the outcome, not the mechanism.

## Proof (the trust engine)

- **Specific beats generic, always.** "Used by 40-room boutique hotels to cut response time 63%" beats "trusted by businesses worldwide."
- **Proof taxonomy:** named testimonials (name, role, company, ideally photo), hard numbers/results, recognizable logos, case studies, ratings/counts, authority/credentials, demonstrations.
- **Seed early, stack late.** A light cue near the hero; the heavy proof immediately before each CTA where doubt peaks.
- **Real only.** Never fabricate logos, testimonials, or numbers. If the user has no proof, say so and design an honest placeholder ("trusted by early teams") rather than inventing one. Honesty is a world-class standard, not a limitation.

## Objections

List the real reasons a qualified visitor says no: price, switching cost, trust, "will it work for my case," time to value, risk. Answer each plainly near the decision point. The biggest objection gets disarmed in the hero or immediately after. An FAQ is the natural home for the rest; write real answers, not deflections.

## CTAs

- **One primary action**, everything serves it. **Repeat it** at decision points (end of hero, after value, after proof, final).
- **Label with action + value:** "Start my free trial", "Get the audit", "Claim my spot." Never "Submit" or "Learn more."
- **Reassurance microcopy** under the button kills last-second friction: "No card required. 2-minute setup. Cancel anytime."
- **Form length tracks commitment:** one field (email) at top of funnel; a few fields for demo/sales; never ask for what you will not use.

## Voice

- Establish voice from the brand source (Phase 0). With none, set it from two adjectives + the audience.
- World-class copy sounds like a sharp human who respects the reader, not a brand brochure. Concrete over abstract, plain over jargon, confident over hype.
- Banned: "revolutionary, seamless, cutting-edge, game-changing, unlock, supercharge, elevate" and similar filler. They signal nothing and read as AI default.
- Read it aloud. If a sentence is hard to say or adds nothing, cut it.

## The cut pass

After drafting, delete ruthlessly. Any sentence that survives deletion without the argument weakening was not needed. Tighten every headline. The best landing-page copy is short because each word fought to stay.
