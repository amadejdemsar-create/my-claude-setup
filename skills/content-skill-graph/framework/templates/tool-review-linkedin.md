# Template: Product/Tool Review (LinkedIn)

> Template for a LinkedIn deep dive post about a product or tool you have personally tested. Translates the technical review into business terms for your LinkedIn audience. Referenced by the **tool-review** workflow.
>
> Voice: [[voice/brand-voice]]
> Audience: [[audience/]] (use the audience profile for your LinkedIn platform)
> Platform: [[platforms/linkedin-post]]

---

## Prerequisites

Same as [[templates/tool-review-x]]: only for products you have personally tested in a real workflow. If the product has not been used hands on, use [[templates/tool-announce-linkedin]] instead.

---

## Post Structure

```
[Hook: practical business problem this product solves]

[Your testing experience in plain language]

[What it means for your audience: concrete use case]

[Honest recommendation: for whom, and why]
```

---

## Hook: Business Problem First

Your audience does not care about the product. They care about their problem. Start there.

**Hook patterns:**

### Time sink hook
> "{{Business task}} used to take me {{time}} every week. For the past {{period}}, I have been using a tool that cuts it down to {{shorter time}}."

### Failed approach hook
> "I tried {{N}} ways to {{task}}. Most did not work. {{Tool}} is the first one that actually solves the problem."

### Before/after hook
> "Before: {{manual process, steps, time}}. Now: {{automated process, steps, time}}. One tool, {{name}}."

### Honest assessment hook
> "{{Tool}} works great for {{specific case}}. For {{other case}}, I would not spend the time."

---

## Testing Experience in Plain Language

Describe what you did with the product in language your audience understands. Translate technical details into operational terms.

**Translation guide (adapt these to your audience):**

| Technical version | Business version |
|---|---|
| "I scraped 200 URLs and fed the markdown into an AI tool for analysis" | "I pulled data from 200 websites and sent it for analysis" |
| "The API returns structured JSON" | "The tool returns organized data that you can immediately use" |
| "I connected it via API to my workflow" | "I connected it to my system so it now works automatically" |
| "Rate limiting on the free tier" | "The free version has a limit on how much you can do per day" |

**What to include:**
- What you used the product for, described in business terms
- How long you tested it
- One specific result that shows the value concretely
- What the product replaced (manual work, other tools, outsourcing)

**What to leave out:**
- API details, technical architecture, developer terminology
- Comparisons with products your audience has never heard of
- Feature by feature breakdowns (this is not a spec sheet)

---

## Business Consequence for Your Audience

This is the core of the post. Connect your testing experience to your reader's reality.

**What matters to your audience (adapt based on [[audience/]]):**
- Can I use this without a developer?
- How much time does it save per week or per month?
- Does it replace something I currently pay for (designer, agency, outsourced service)?
- Is it available in my language or at least understandable enough?
- What does it cost, and is that realistic for my business size?

**Framing patterns:**

> "For a company with {{size}} employees that does {{task}} weekly, this means {{concrete consequence: hours saved, less manual work, lower costs}}."

> "If you currently {{current approach}}, with this tool you would instead {{new approach}}. The difference is {{concrete difference}}."

> "Practical example: {{scenario from everyday work that your reader would recognize}}."

---

## Honest Recommendation

Clear, specific, and honest. Say who should use it AND who should not.

**Recommendation patterns:**

> "I recommend it if {{condition}}. Not worth it if {{other condition}}."

> "For {{type of business}}, this is a worthwhile investment. For {{other type}}, simpler solutions exist."

> "Try the free version and see if it works for your case. The paid version pays for itself only at {{volume of work}}."

**What makes an honest recommendation:**
- It names a specific type of business or role, not "everyone"
- It acknowledges real limitations
- It mentions the cost and whether it makes sense at your audience's scale
- If the free tier is enough for most of your audience, say that

---

## Voice Notes

**Sound like:** A colleague who tested something and is sharing an honest opinion over coffee. Not selling. Not teaching. Not trying to impress.

**Language rules (if writing in a non-English language, per [[config.md]]):**
- Write in natural word order for the target language (not translated English)
- Use everyday words your audience actually uses
- Avoid loanwords when native equivalents exist and are commonly used
- One idea per sentence
- Short paragraphs (2 to 3 sentences max)

**What to avoid:**
- Agency style language ("optimization of your processes with innovative solutions")
- Superlatives ("the best tool I have ever used")
- Pretending every business needs this ("every entrepreneur must have this")
- Technical jargon without explanation
- Hype ("revolutionary," "mind blowing," "unbelievable")

**What to use:**
- "I tested this myself..." (first person, grounded)
- "In practice it looks like this..." (concrete, real)
- "Honest impression: ..." (honest framing)
- "For my use case..." (qualified, not universal)

See [[voice/brand-voice]] for your complete voice guidelines.

## Example

```
Preparing content for social media took me 3 to 4 hours a week. Finding inspiration, adjusting copy, creating graphics.

For the past 2 months I have been using Firecrawl, a tool that lets me pull content from any website and use it for analysis in seconds. Before, I did this manually: open the page, copy, paste, format. Now the same result comes from a single command.

For a company that regularly monitors competitors or collects information from the web (prices, offers, news), this means that instead of an hour of manual work you spend 5 minutes. The result is the same, it just arrives faster.

I recommend it if you regularly collect data from the web for your work. The free version is enough for smaller volumes. The paid version pays for itself if you do this daily or at larger scale.
```
