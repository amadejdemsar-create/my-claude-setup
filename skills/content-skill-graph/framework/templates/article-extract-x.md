# Template: Article Extract (Short Form / X)

> Template for a shorter post extracted from a published blog article. Each extract takes one insight, stat, or argument from the article and turns it into a standalone post. Referenced by the **article-repurpose** workflow.
>
> Voice: [[voice/brand-voice]]
> Audience: [[audience/]] (use the audience profile for your short form platform)
> Platform: [[platforms/x-post]] or equivalent short form platform

---

## Post Structure

```
[Hook: the most striking insight, stat, or claim from the article, presented as standalone]

[2 to 4 sentences expanding on this single point]

[Natural curiosity bridge to the full article, not a hard sell]

Full article: {{link}}
```

---

## Extraction Criteria

Not every paragraph makes a good extract. The best extracts share these qualities:

**What makes a good extract:**
- A surprising statistic that challenges assumptions ("88% adopt, 1% mature")
- A counterintuitive claim that makes someone pause ("I stopped optimizing my prompts. My output quality went up.")
- An actionable takeaway someone can use immediately
- A strong analogy that reframes a familiar concept ("Excel was built for accountants...")
- A specific personal result with concrete details
- A contrarian position backed by evidence

**What does NOT make a good extract:**
- A summary of the article (that is a teaser, not an extract)
- A generic observation without a sharp angle
- A point that requires reading the article to understand
- A list of features or tools without a connecting insight

**The critical test:** Would this post be valuable if the reader never clicks the link? If yes, it is a good extract. If it feels incomplete without the article, it needs more substance.

## Hook Formulas by Extract Type

### For statistic extracts
> "{{Stat}}. {{What most people assume instead}}. {{Why the gap exists, in one sentence}}."

> "{{Number}} {{of what}}. {{Context that makes the number surprising}}."

### For counterintuitive claim extracts
> "{{The unexpected action or result}}. {{Why this contradicts conventional wisdom}}."

> "Everyone {{does X}}. I {{did Y instead}}. {{What happened}}."

### For analogy extracts
> "{{Familiar situation}} is exactly what {{the parallel situation}} looks like."

> "{{Analogy setup}}. {{What it illustrates about the topic}}."

### For actionable takeaway extracts
> "{{The specific practice}}. {{Why it works, briefly}}. {{What changes as a result}}."

---

## Expanding the Point

After the hook, add 2 to 4 sentences that develop the idea enough to stand alone.

**What to include:**
- The "so what" for the reader's situation
- One concrete example or evidence point
- The implication or consequence of this insight

**What to leave out:**
- Background context the reader does not need for this single point
- Multiple arguments (one per post)
- References to other points in the article ("As I also discuss...")

---

## Bridge to the Full Article

The link should feel natural, not like a call to action. The reader just consumed a complete, valuable thought. The article is where they go if they want more depth on the broader topic.

**Good bridges:**
- "I wrote about this and {{X related points}} in the full piece: {{link}}"
- "This is one of {{N}} things I unpacked in {{article topic}}: {{link}}"
- "Full article covers {{broader topic}}: {{link}}"

**Bad bridges:**
- "Read more here!" (empty urgency)
- "Click the link to learn the rest" (implies the post is incomplete)
- "Thread continues below" (this is not a thread)

## Voice Notes

Follow your [[voice/brand-voice]] guidelines:
- Direct, specific, first person singular
- Technical when the detail matters
- Slightly skeptical of hype, comfortable with rough edges
- No superlatives, no FOMO, no motivational framing

## Example

**Extract from a stats insight:**

```
88% of organizations use AI in at least one function.
Only 1% believe they have reached maturity.
95% of enterprise AI pilots fail.

The gap is not about which model they picked. It is about whether anyone organized the context the model needs before deploying it. Giving employees an AI subscription without structuring what the tool should know about the business is like buying laptops without installing any software.

This is one of the core arguments in the full piece: https://yourbrand.com/blog/article-slug
```

**Extract from an analogy:**

```
Excel was built for accountants. Today marketers, PMs, HR, and ops people use it more than accountants do.

The same shift is happening with agentic AI tools. These tools read files, write files, execute commands, follow instructions, and connect to external services. None of that requires you to know how to code. The word "Code" in the name is a packaging decision, not a capability description.

I wrote about why the naming problem is costing these tools 90% of their potential user base: https://yourbrand.com/blog/article-slug
```
