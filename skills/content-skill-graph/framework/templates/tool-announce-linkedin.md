# Template: Product/Tool Announcement (LinkedIn)

> Template for announcing a new product or tool on LinkedIn. Reframes the product in business terms for your LinkedIn audience. Referenced by the **tool-announce** workflow.
>
> Voice: [[voice/brand-voice]]
> Audience: [[audience/]] (use the audience profile for your LinkedIn platform)
> Platform: [[platforms/linkedin-post]]

---

## Post Structure

```
[Hook: what business problem this product solves, in plain language]

[2 to 3 sentences: what the product does explained without jargon, as if telling a colleague over coffee]

[Why the reader should care: concrete use case, time savings, or workflow change]

[Optional: link to your review page or the product's official page]
```

---

## Hook Formulas

### 1. Problem hook
Start with a frustration or time sink the audience recognizes.

> "Every week you spend hours on {{manual task}}. {{Tool}} now does that in minutes."

> "{{Business pain point}} is a problem everyone who runs a {{type of business}} knows. A new tool just shipped that solves it."

### 2. Concrete result hook
Lead with a specific, tangible outcome.

> "{{Tool}} produces {{concrete result}} in {{timeframe}}. No technical knowledge required."

> "Before, {{task}} took {{time}}. Now it is done in {{shorter time}}."

### 3. "This just changed" hook
Signal that something shifted, but without theatrics.

> "{{Company}} released {{tool}} this week. For {{type of user}}, this means {{concrete effect}}."

---

## Body: Plain Language Explanation

Explain what the product does as if the person has never heard of it and does not follow tech news.

**Rules for writing:**
- Write in natural sentence structure for your target language (see [[config.md]])
- Use words your audience actually uses in conversation
- Avoid loanwords and jargon when native equivalents exist and are commonly used
- One idea per sentence
- If a technical term is necessary, explain it immediately in the same sentence

**What to cover:**
- What the tool concretely does (in one sentence)
- Who it is for (role or business type, not persona jargon)
- One specific thing it enables that was previously difficult, slow, or expensive

---

## Business Consequence

This is the part that makes the post valuable for the audience. Translate the technical capability into operational language.

**Good framing patterns:**
- "This means that {{role}} instead of {{manual process}} now {{automated process}}."
- "For a company with {{size/type}}, this means {{concrete consequence}}."
- "In practice: {{scenario from real work the reader would recognize}}."

**What matters to your audience (adapt based on [[audience/]]):**
- Time saved on repetitive tasks
- Fewer handoffs between people
- Lower cost for something they currently outsource
- Simpler way to do something they already need to do
- Reduced dependency on specialists or agencies

## Link (Optional)

```
Full overview: {{link-to-your-review-or-product-page}}
```

Only include the link if the page exists and adds value beyond the post itself. A link to a page that says less than the post does not help.

---

## Voice Notes

**Sound like:** A calm, practical person explaining something they saw this week. Not selling, not teaching, not inspiring. Just sharing something useful in plain language.

**What to avoid:**
- Agency style inspiration posts ("The future is now!")
- Corporate words nobody uses in real conversation ("implementation of solutions for process optimization")
- Superlatives and hype ("the best tool ever," "revolutionary")
- Pretending every business needs this tool
- Trying to sound futuristic or visionary

**Do use:**
- Everyday language ("spend hours," "manual work," "without knowing how")
- Specific examples from recognizable business contexts
- Honest qualifiers ("if you have {{type of business}}," "for those who frequently {{task}}")
- First person when relevant ("I tested this myself," "In practice it looks like this")

See [[voice/brand-voice]] for your complete voice guidelines.

## Example

```
Creating visual content for social media, posters, or invitations is time consuming for most small businesses. You either do it yourself in Canva (and spend half a day), or you pay a designer.

Google released Nano Banana 2 this week. You describe what you need, and in 3 seconds you get an image. It also handles text on images, which means posters and graphics actually contain readable text.

For a business that prepares weekly posts for Instagram or Facebook, this means that instead of one hour in Canva you spend 10 minutes in conversation with the tool. The free version works globally.

Full overview: https://yourbrand.com/tools/nano-banana
```

## Update Variant

When announcing a **product update** (not a new product), the framing shifts from introducing the product to explaining what changed and what it means for someone running a business.

### Post Structure (Update)

```
[Hook: what changed, framed as a business consequence]

[2 to 3 sentences: what the tool can do now that it could not do before. Keep it concrete, no feature lists.]

[Business consequence: what this means for someone who might already use the tool, or who should now consider it]

[Optional: link to review page]
```

### Hook Formulas (Update)

#### 1. Before/after hook
> "{{Tool}} used to {{old limitation}}. After yesterday's update, {{new capability}}."

> "Before, {{tool}} could only {{old functionality}}. Now it {{new functionality}}."

#### 2. Consequence hook
> "If you use {{tool}}, yesterday's update changes what you can do with it."

> "{{Company}} updated {{tool}}. For {{type of user}}, this means {{concrete change}}."

### Body: What Changed, Simply

Explain the update as if the reader may or may not know the tool. One sentence of orientation ("{{Tool}} is {{short description}}"), then focus on what is new.

**Rules:**
- Do not list every change; pick the 2 to 3 that matter most for your audience
- Frame each change as a practical consequence, not a feature name
- If pricing changed, mention it explicitly

### Example (Update)

```
Google Stitch used to be a text to interface generator. After yesterday's update, it became a tool for designing entire applications.

Instead of static HTML files, it now generates working React applications. It has a voice mode where you talk to the tool and it adjusts the design in real time. A new "design agent" remembers the entire project history and suggests improvements based on past decisions.

For a business that hires designers or developers for simple internal applications, this means you can prepare a first version yourself in one afternoon. The free version is available globally.

Updated overview: https://yourbrand.com/tools/stitch
```
