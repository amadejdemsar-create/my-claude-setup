# Hormozi Standard

> The quality bar for your content: your free content should be better than the competition's paid content. Every guide, tutorial, and deep dive should make someone think "I cannot believe this is free." This is the trust engine that replaces case studies, testimonials, and brand recognition when you have none of those yet.

References: [[quality/calibration]], [[engine/power-law]], [[voice/brand-voice]]

---

## The Test

If someone could buy a $97 course and get less value than this free guide, post, or article, the content passes.

If they would get roughly the same value, the content needs more depth, more specifics, or more actionable steps.

If they would get more value from the paid course, the content should not be published under your brand name until it is improved.

This is not about length. A 200 word post can pass the Hormozi standard if it contains one genuinely useful insight that the reader cannot easily find elsewhere. A 5,000 word blog article can fail it if every section says things the reader has already heard.

## What "Better Than Paid" Looks Like

### Specific, actionable steps (not theory)

Paid courses sell frameworks. Your free content gives the implementation. Instead of "create a system for managing context," the content says: "Create a file called X in your project root. Add these sections. Here is a real example from a production project." The reader can do the thing after reading the content, not just understand the concept.

### Real examples from actual work (not hypotheticals)

Paid courses use sanitized, simplified examples designed for teaching. Your content uses messy, real examples from actual projects, complete with the constraints, tradeoffs, and unexpected complications that real work involves. "Here is how this actually works, including the parts that were harder than expected and the workarounds that were needed" is more valuable than a clean textbook example.

### Copy paste deliverables

The most valuable content includes things the reader can directly use: exact prompts that produce good results, exact configuration files they can adapt, exact folder structures they can replicate, exact command sequences they can run. The reader should not have to translate the content into action; the action should be in the content.

### Honest limitations alongside recommendations

Paid courses often sell a single approach as the best approach. Your content includes the limitations, the edge cases where the approach does not work, and the situations where a different approach would be better. "Tool X is excellent for A and B, but struggles with C, and for C you should use Y instead" builds more trust than unconditional enthusiasm.

### Depth that teaches rather than teases

Paid courses use free content as a teaser: give enough to demonstrate expertise but withhold the actual method so people pay for the full version. Your content gives the full method for free. The entire point is that the free content is the product. There is nothing being held back.

## What Fails the Hormozi Test

### Vague overviews that could be on any blog

"AI is transforming how businesses operate. Here are five ways companies are using AI." This content exists on a thousand other blogs. It contains no original insight, no real experience, and no actionable information. The reader learns nothing they could not have found with a two minute web search.

### Lists of tips without implementation detail

"Tip 3: Use AI to automate your reporting." Without showing exactly which tool, exactly which configuration, exactly which prompts, and exactly what the output looks like, this is a suggestion, not a guide. Suggestions are cheap. Implementation details are valuable.

### "10 ways to use X" without showing a single real result

Listicles that enumerate possibilities without demonstrating that the author has actually done any of them. The reader has no way to assess whether these suggestions actually work because the author has not shown their own results.

### Content that leaves the reader thinking "okay, but how exactly?"

The clearest sign of failing the Hormozi test. If the reader finishes the content and still does not know how to do the thing, the content teased rather than taught. Every piece of your content should leave the reader with enough information to take the next step, even if that step is small.

## Scope: When the Hormozi Standard Applies

### Big swing posts and blog articles: full standard

These are the pieces that build reputation. Long form guides, deep dive tutorials, workflow breakdowns, and pillar content all must pass the Hormozi test. If the content is going to carry your brand name and represent your expertise, it needs to be extraordinary.

### Fast volume posts: adjusted standard

Quick observations, content repurposing, short commentary, and rapid response posts operate under different rules. They need to be good: specific, honest, and useful. They do not need to be exhaustive. A fast post can be a single observation with a concrete detail, and that is enough. The Hormozi standard applies to pieces where depth is the point; fast posts serve different purposes (visibility, engagement, community building).

### Internal documents and strategy: not applicable

Strategy docs, planning documents, and internal notes do not need to meet this bar. They serve operational purposes, not audience facing purposes.

## Why This Matters (Especially Early On)

When you are building a brand without established social proof, your content IS the proof.

When someone discovers you for the first time, the only evidence they have is the content itself. If that content is generic, surface level, or indistinguishable from what everyone else in your space publishes, there is no reason to believe your paid services or products are any better.

But if the free content is extraordinary, if it teaches real skills with real examples and real depth, the reader concludes: "If this is what they give away for free, the paid work must be incredible." This conclusion is the entire sales engine until you have traditional social proof (case studies, testimonials, brand recognition) to supplement it.

Every piece of content that passes the Hormozi standard builds that trust. Every piece that fails it undermines it. The standard is not about perfectionism; it is about earning the right to charge for services by proving competence through free work.
