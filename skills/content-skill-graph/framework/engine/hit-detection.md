# Hit Detection and Compounding

> How to identify when a post significantly outperforms your baseline and what to do about it. The power law rewards compounding: a hit is not a lucky break to move on from, it is a signal to lean into.

References: [[engine/power-law]], [[engine/scheduling]], [[engine/content-repurposing]]

---

## Tracking

Track engagement on every post across all your active platforms (configured in [[config]]). The metrics that matter:

- **Likes** (baseline signal of resonance)
- **Comments** (stronger signal; indicates the post provoked a reaction worth typing)
- **Reposts/shares** (strongest signal; the reader endorsed the post to their own audience)
- **Profile views** (lagging indicator; spikes after a hit and shows the post made someone curious about you)

Establish a **rolling average over the last 20 posts** on each platform. This gives you a stable baseline that adjusts as your account grows. Separate the averages for each platform because the engagement scales are completely different.

---

## Hit Signal

**A post is a hit when it reaches 3x your rolling average engagement.**

Use total engagement (likes + comments + reposts) as the primary metric. If profile views spike significantly alongside a high-engagement post, that confirms the hit reached beyond your existing audience.

Do not wait for a post to go "viral" by some absolute standard. 3x your own baseline is what matters, because it tells you that this specific topic or format resonated disproportionately with your specific audience. A post with 45 engagements is a hit if your rolling average is 15.

---

## Response Protocol (Within 48 Hours)

When you identify a hit, respond quickly. The algorithm and audience attention are both time-sensitive. Within 48 hours of recognizing the hit, execute as many of the following as fit:

### 1. Follow-up post going deeper

Expand the angle from the hit post. Add a new example, go into more detail on the mechanism, or answer questions that came up in the comments. Do not repeat the original post; build on it. The audience already resonated with the topic; now give them more depth.

### 2. Consider turning it into a series

If the topic has enough depth, plan a sequence:
- "Part 2" the following week with a different facet of the same idea.
- A longer article version on your website or blog that fully develops the argument.
- A guide or tutorial if the hit was about a practical technique.

A series keeps the topic alive and signals to the algorithm that your account produces sustained, related content rather than one-off spikes.

### 3. Extract the core topic for business use

The topic that hit reveals what the audience cares about. Consider:
- **Product/service angle:** Could this become the core topic or opening example for a paid offering (workshop, course, consulting engagement)?
- **Lead magnet idea:** Could a deeper version of this (checklist, template, mini-guide) work as a free resource that captures emails?
- **Outbound conversation starter:** Could this insight open a DM conversation with a potential client? ("I noticed you commented on my post about X; here is something related I have been working on...")

### 4. If the hit was a content asset repurposing post

When a post based on existing content assets (spotlight, comparison, category roundup) hits, the format resonated as much as the content. Create more posts using the same template with different subjects. If a comparison format hit, do three more comparisons that week. If a spotlight on a specific category hit, spotlight other items in that category.

---

## What Not to Do After a Hit

**Do not treat it as a one-off lucky break and immediately return to the scheduled plan.** This is the most common mistake. The hit tells you something important about what the audience wants to hear about, and the power law rewards the creators who compound that signal rather than abandoning it.

**Do not copy the exact post format and fill in different words.** The audience will notice. The follow-up needs to add genuine new thinking, not just recycle the structure with a different subject or number.

**Do not wait a week to follow up.** The attention window is short. If you wait too long, the momentum dissipates and the follow-up feels disconnected from the original.

**Do not over-analyze why it hit before you respond.** You can analyze later. Right now, the priority is to compound while the signal is fresh. Perfect understanding of why something worked is less valuable than fast, good-enough follow-up content.

---

## When to Stop Compounding

Compound until engagement on the follow-up posts returns to your baseline rolling average. That is the signal that the audience has absorbed what you had to offer on this topic and is ready for something new.

If the topic sustains above-average engagement across three or more follow-up posts, consider making it a permanent content category rather than a temporary series.

---

## Updating the Rolling Average

After a hit and its follow-up posts, your rolling average will naturally increase. This is good. It means your baseline expectation rises, and future hits need to clear a higher bar. This prevents you from resting on early wins and keeps pushing the content quality upward.
