# Content Asset Repurposing

> The 80% fast volume posts. If you have a website, knowledge base, content library, or any structured repository of information, you do not need to invent content from scratch every day. Repurpose what already exists. Each template below produces a post in under 15 minutes.

References: [[engine/power-law]], [[voice/brand-voice]], [[config]], [[pillars/]]

---

## Source Data

Identify the existing content assets you can draw from. These are any structured repositories of information you already maintain:

- **Website pages:** Product pages, service descriptions, case studies, blog posts, landing pages, resource libraries.
- **Knowledge base:** Internal docs, guides, how-tos, FAQs, playbooks, wikis.
- **Data collections:** Tool lists, comparisons, directories, catalogs, inventories.
- **Past content:** Previous articles, newsletters, presentations, webinar transcripts.
- **Project work:** Client deliverables (anonymized), internal projects, experiments, prototypes.

During onboarding (see [[config]]), map your content assets so the system knows where to pull from. The more structured the source data, the faster the repurposing.

## Template 1: Item Spotlight (Quick)

One item from your content library, three sentences: what it is, who it is for, one surprising detail. Works across platforms with tone adjusted per [[voice/brand-voice]].

**Time to produce:** 5 to 10 minutes.

**Structure:**
1. Name the item and what it does/is in one sentence.
2. Who benefits most from it (specific role, team size, or use case, not "everyone").
3. One aspect that most people overlook or do not know about.

**Visual (optional):** Simple card with icon/image, name, and one-liner. Use your brand colors and format.

---

## Template 2: Deep Dive

One item broken down in depth: what it is, key features or aspects, who should care, who should skip it. Longer post with a visual showing the breakdown.

**Time to produce:** 10 to 15 minutes.

**Structure:**
1. What the item actually does or offers (not the marketing pitch; the real value).
2. Three to five key features, aspects, or details that matter in practice.
3. Who should use it or care about it (specific use case or profile).
4. Who should skip it (honest about limitations).
5. One opinion or recommendation.

**Business platform adaptation:** Focus on "what this means for a small team" and avoid jargon. Translate features into business consequences: time saved, process simplified, fewer handoffs.

**Visual (optional):** Feature breakdown card using your brand format.

---

## Template 3: Head-to-Head Comparison

Two similar items side by side: when to choose which, key differences, clear recommendation. Your content library likely has natural matchups.

**Time to produce:** 10 to 15 minutes.

**Identifying natural matchups:** Look at your content assets for items that serve similar purposes, compete in the same category, or are frequently confused with each other. These are your comparison candidates.

**Structure:**
1. Name both items and the category or problem they address.
2. Two to three key differences that actually matter (not marketing bullet points).
3. When to pick Item A over Item B (specific scenario).
4. When to pick Item B over Item A (specific scenario).
5. Clear verdict: which one and why.

**Visual (optional):** Comparison card with both items, key differentiators, and a verdict. Two-column layout works well.

---

## Template 4: Category Roundup

Best items for a specific use case or category. Pull from your content library. Keep it opinionated; rank them or pick a winner.

**Time to produce:** 10 to 15 minutes.

**Structure:**
1. Name the category and the use case or problem.
2. List three to five items, each with one sentence explaining why it is included.
3. Pick a winner or provide a clear recommendation for different situations.
4. One item to watch (something newer or underrated).

**Visual (optional):** Category overview card with icons and one-liner each. Horizontal layout works well for three to five items.

---

## Template 5: Practical Business Example

Pick one item from your content assets, explain in plain language what it does and why a business of a specific size should care. Zero jargon. Focus on time saved or process simplified.

**Time to produce:** 10 to 15 minutes.

**Structure:**
1. Name the item.
2. What it does, explained as if talking to someone who has never heard of it.
3. One specific example of how a team of a specific size would use it.
4. What changes in their week (time, fewer errors, less manual work, clearer information).
5. What it costs and whether the ROI makes sense at that scale.

**This template exists specifically for audiences that do not follow your niche closely.** The language should sound like a peer explaining something over coffee, not like a product review.

**Visual (optional):** Simple card in the audience's language using your brand format.

## Visual Specs

If you create visuals for repurposed content, define your specifications here during onboarding. Key decisions:

| Decision | What to define |
|----------|---------------|
| Dimensions | Standard card sizes for each platform (e.g., 1584x396, 1080x1080) |
| Brand elements | Colors, fonts, logo placement from your brand guide |
| Export method | Your tool chain for creating and exporting visuals |
| Retina support | Whether to export at 2x for high-DPI displays |
| Layouts | Standard layouts for spotlights, comparisons, roundups |

Document your visual specs in [[config]] or a separate visual guide so the system can generate consistent assets.

## Workflow

1. Pick an item or pair of items from your content assets.
2. Read the source material for structured information.
3. Choose the template that fits (spotlight for quick posts, comparison when two items compete, roundup when a category angle is fresh).
4. Write the post text. Each platform version is written separately, not translated. See [[engine/repurpose-rules]].
5. Create the visual using your defined toolchain (if applicable).
6. Export the visual.
7. Post. Under 15 minutes total.

---

## Building Your Content Asset Library

If you do not yet have structured content assets to pull from, start building the library incrementally:

1. **Audit what you already have.** Most creators underestimate how much structured content they already possess: website pages, internal docs, past presentations, email sequences, client deliverables.
2. **Structure it.** The more structured the data (categories, tags, descriptions, use cases), the faster the repurposing. Even a simple spreadsheet with columns for name, category, one-liner, and key features is enough to start.
3. **Add to it as you go.** Every new project, client interaction, or experiment produces content that can be cataloged and repurposed later.
4. **Connect it to your content pillars.** Map each asset to the content pillars defined in [[pillars/]] so you know which topics each asset can serve.
