# Post Templates

> Fill-in post templates for builder/technical platforms and hook principles with annotated examples for business/professional platforms. Templates are starting structures, not rigid formulas. The hook must earn the next line. Every template opens with something specific enough that a reader stops scrolling.

References: [[engine/hooks]], [[voice/brand-voice]], [[config]]

---

## Builder/Technical Platform Templates

These templates work well on platforms where the audience is hands-on with tools and technology (X/Twitter, dev communities, technical forums). Adapt the tone to match your [[voice/brand-voice]] settings.

### Template 1: The Uncomfortable Number

Open with a number that makes the reader feel something. The number anchors the entire post and creates the tension that carries through to the insight.

```
[Specific number] hours.

That is how long [specific person or team] spent on [specific task]
last [week/month] before I [what you did].

Now it takes [new number].

The interesting part is not the automation.
It is what [person/team] does with those [number] hours back.
[One sentence on what changed downstream.]
```

**Why it works:** The number in the first line stops the scroll. The before/after creates a gap the reader wants explained. The closing reframes the insight from "automation is cool" to "what happens after automation," which is where the real thinking lives.

---

### Template 2: The Assumption Breaker

State a common belief as if it is obviously true, then show where it falls apart. The reader stays because you challenged something they accepted without questioning.

```
[Common belief stated as fact].

Except that falls apart when you [specific thing you tried or observed].

I [did specific thing] and what actually happened was [unexpected result].
The reason: [short explanation that reframes the original belief].

Most people never test this because [why the assumption persists].
```

**Why it works:** The first line feels familiar and safe. The second line breaks it. The personal experience makes the reframe credible. The closing explains why others have not seen this, which validates the reader for not having caught it themselves.

---

### Template 3: The Live Walkthrough

Start in the middle of action. No preamble, no context setting. The reader is dropped into a process and follows along.

```
Here is what happened when I [specific action] this morning.

[Step 1: what you did, what tool, what it produced]
[Step 2: what came back, what surprised you]
[Step 3: the result]

Total time: [number].
The same thing used to take [old number] because [why].

If you are still [doing old way], the difference is not speed.
It is that [deeper insight about what changes].
```

**Why it works:** "This morning" signals recency and realness. The step by step format is easy to scan. The time comparison is concrete proof. The closing elevates the post from "look what I did" to "here is what this means for you."

---

### Template 4: The Question That Reframes

Take a question the audience is already asking and offer a better version that reveals the hidden assumption in the original.

```
Everyone is asking [popular question in your field].

Better question: [your reframe].

Because [popular question] assumes [hidden assumption].
Once you drop that assumption, the answer changes completely.
[2 to 3 sentences explaining what you see instead.]
```

**Why it works:** The reader recognizes the popular question and feels included. The reframe shifts their perspective. The explanation of the hidden assumption gives them something they can use in their own thinking.

---

### Template 5: The Side by Side

Same conditions, different results. This format eliminates every variable except the one you want the reader to focus on.

```
Same task. Same model. Two completely different results.

Version A: [describe blank prompt / no context approach and what it produced]
Version B: [describe context-rich approach and what it produced]

The model did not get smarter between A and B.
The only thing that changed was [what you gave it / how you set it up].
That is the part most people skip.
```

**Why it works:** The controlled comparison is the most convincing format because the reader cannot attribute the difference to anything other than the variable you highlight. The closing line points directly at the action they should take.

## Business/Professional Platform: Hook Principles and Examples

Fill-in-the-blank templates often underperform on business platforms where the audience is experienced enough to recognize formulas. Instead of rigid structures, follow the principles below and study the annotated examples.

Adapt the language and cultural references to your target audience as defined in [[audience/]] and [[config]].

### What Stops the Scroll

- A number in the first line: hours, currency, a percentage. Numbers are concrete in a sea of abstract posts.
- Something the reader recognizes from their own week but has never seen someone articulate clearly.
- A short story that starts in the middle. Drop the reader into a meeting, a conversation, a decision. Do not set the scene first.
- A claim that goes against what everyone is currently saying, backed by a specific observation.

### What Gets Skipped

- "I keep seeing the same pattern at companies" and any variation. This opener signals a lecture, not a story.
- Opening with a question ("Have you ever wondered..."). The reader answers "no" internally and moves on.
- Starting with context instead of the point. If the first two lines are setup, nobody reaches the payoff.
- Anything that sounds like it was written for an audience rather than to one person.

---

### Example 1: The Number That Stings

> 14 hours.
>
> That is how long one person on the team spent last week manually copying the same data from one system to another.
>
> Nobody told them to. It had just always been done that way. Nobody ever asked why.
>
> When I showed them it could be automated in 20 minutes, they went quiet for about 10 seconds. Then they said: "Why did nobody tell me this earlier?"
>
> Most companies do not have a problem with AI. They have a problem with nobody looking at where the hours actually disappear.

**Why it works:** Opens with a number that makes you wince. Tells a specific story with an emotional moment (the 10 seconds of silence). Closes with an observation the reader can apply to their own company. The AI mention is incidental; the post is about wasted time.

---

### Example 2: The Story That Starts in the Middle

> In the middle of a meeting, the director asked me: "But what if AI makes a mistake?"
>
> I did not answer immediately. Instead, I asked how many errors the current process produces. He looked at the data. The answer was: roughly 8% of orders have at least one manual error.
>
> Then we looked at how many of those errors would be caught if the data flowed automatically instead of through three spreadsheets and two emails.
>
> The conversation changed quite a bit after that.
>
> The fear of AI mistakes is often bigger than the awareness of how many mistakes are already happening without it.

**Why it works:** Drops the reader into a meeting. The dialogue is instantly recognizable; every business owner has heard this objection. The counter-question is the real move, and the reader learns a reframing technique they can use themselves. No jargon. No tools mentioned. Pure business conversation.

---

### Example 3: The Uncomfortable Comparison

> Yesterday I prepared a full competitive analysis, summary of key differences, a positioning proposal, and drafts of three emails for a client.
>
> It took 35 minutes.
>
> A year ago the same thing would have taken a full day, and the result would have been worse, because after four hours I would have already lost focus.
>
> The difference is not that I became faster. The difference is that I gave the system enough context to understand what kind of business this is, what they sell, who they sell to, and how they communicate.
>
> Without that context, the same system produces generic text you have to rewrite anyway. With context, it produces something you actually use.

**Why it works:** Concrete deliverables with an exact time. The year-ago comparison is specific (mentions losing focus after four hours, which every reader has experienced). The insight is about context, not about AI being fast. The reader imagines applying this to their own work.

---

### Example 4: The Quiet Provocation

> A company that hires someone new today and gives them a laptop with no documented process, no recorded knowledge, and no clear structure is going to be in serious trouble in five years.
>
> Not because AI is better than people.
>
> Because the competition that organized their knowledge and made it accessible to their systems will operate with half the team at twice the speed.
>
> The question is not whether your company will use AI. The question is whether your knowledge will be written down somewhere the system can read it, or whether it will stay in the heads of three people who will eventually leave.

**Why it works:** Opens with a scenario every business owner recognizes (new hire, no documentation). The provocation is quiet rather than aggressive; it describes a consequence rather than issuing a warning. The closing reframe from "will you use AI" to "will your knowledge be accessible" is the kind of shift that sticks with someone for days.

## Adapting Templates to Your Brand

When using these templates:

1. Replace generic examples with real outcomes from your work. See [[pillars/]] for your content topics.
2. Match the tone to your [[voice/brand-voice]] for each platform.
3. Adjust the technical depth based on your [[audience/]] profiles. Builder audiences want mechanism and specifics. Business audiences want consequence and clarity.
4. If you post in multiple languages, write each version from scratch rather than translating. See [[engine/repurpose-rules]].
