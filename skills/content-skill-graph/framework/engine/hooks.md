# Hook Formulas

> Hook types that earn the next line on social platforms. A hook is not a headline; it is the first sentence or two that make someone stop scrolling and commit to reading the rest. Every hook here has been filtered against the anti-patterns that mark generic AI content accounts.

References: [[voice/brand-voice]], [[engine/templates]], [[config]]

---

## Hook Type 1: The Specific Result

Open with a concrete outcome, then explain how you got there. The number or result does the heavy lifting. The reader stays because they want to know the mechanism.

**Structure:** [Concrete outcome with a number or specific detail]. Then [how it happened in one sentence].

**Examples:**

1. "35 minutes. That is how long it took to produce a full competitive analysis, positioning draft, and three outreach emails for a client. A year ago the same task was a full day."
2. "I deleted 14 hours of weekly manual data entry with a scraper I built in one evening. The scraper is ugly. The 14 hours are gone."
3. "Built 27 custom automations across our stack with no dedicated engineering team. The bottleneck was never technical ability; it was clarity of thought."

When using this hook type, replace the numbers and tasks with outcomes from your own work. Pull from real projects and real time savings. See [[config]] for your domain and [[audience/]] for what outcomes resonate with your readers.

**Anti-examples (what to avoid):**

1. "I saved SO much time with AI this week!" (No specifics, no number, no mechanism. Could be any account.)
2. "Here's how I 10x'd my productivity" (Vague multiplier with no anchoring detail. Reads like a growth hacker thread.)
3. "The results were insane" (Empty superlative. Tells the reader nothing about what happened.)

## Hook Type 2: The Counterintuitive Observation

State something that goes against what most people believe, then immediately back it up with evidence or experience. The reader stays because you challenged their assumption and they want to see if you can defend it.

**Structure:** [Common belief or expectation]. Except [what actually happens when you test it or look closely].

**Examples:**

1. "Everyone is comparing models. I spent the last month organizing context. That is where the actual output quality comes from."
2. "Adding AI to a broken process does not fix the process. It makes the broken parts more visible and moves the bottleneck somewhere nobody expected."
3. "The fastest way to build a content engine is not to post more. It is to figure out what you actually think about three topics and say it clearly."

Ground counterintuitive observations in your own experience. The claim must be something you can defend with specifics from your work. Check [[philosophy]] for your core beliefs and [[pillars/]] for the topics where you have genuine contrarian takes.

**Anti-examples (what to avoid):**

1. "Unpopular opinion: AI is overrated" (Contrarian for engagement, not for insight. No evidence, no experience, no follow through.)
2. "Everyone is wrong about AI agents" (Broad claim with no specific target. The reader has no reason to believe you over the crowd.)

## Hook Type 3: The Honest Comparison

Same task, different approaches, real results. The side by side eliminates every variable except the one that matters. The reader stays because the comparison makes the insight undeniable.

**Structure:** Same [task/model/tool]. [Approach A and its result]. [Approach B and its result]. The difference was [the one variable that changed].

**Examples:**

1. "Same task. Same model. Two completely different results. Version A got a generic five paragraph essay. Version B got a draft I actually used. The only difference was what the model knew about my business before I typed a word."
2. "Two companies, same industry, same tools. One cut reporting time by 80%. The other added a chatbot to their website and called it a day. The gap was not budget. It was how they organized their knowledge."
3. "I wrote the same post twice: once by following a template word for word, once by starting from the raw idea and writing from experience. The template version read like a robot. The second version got 4x the engagement."

Use comparisons from your own projects and experiments. The more controlled the comparison (same tools, same timeframe, different approach), the more convincing. Reference specific details from your work.

**Anti-examples (what to avoid):**

1. "Tool A vs Tool B: which one wins?" (Clickbait framing. No personal experience, no specific task, no real comparison.)
2. "Before AI vs After AI" (Too broad. Every AI account posts this. There is no specific detail to learn from.)
3. "I tested GPT vs Claude and the results were shocking" (The word "shocking" signals that the comparison will be shallow and the conclusion predetermined.)

## Hook Type 4: The Builder's Walkthrough

Start mid-process. Do not set up the context first; drop the reader into the action and let them piece together what is happening. The reader stays because they are already inside the story.

**Structure:** Here is what happened when I [specific action] [timeframe]. [Step or detail]. [What came back or surprised you].

**Examples:**

1. "Here is what happened when I pointed an AI agent at our entire knowledge base this morning. First output: a strategy document that referenced client conversations from three months ago."
2. "I wired an automation tool into our database last Tuesday. By Wednesday it had already caught two data mismatches that had been sitting there for weeks."
3. "Built a scraper at 11pm because I got tired of watching four hours vanish into copy paste. Version one was ugly. It cut the job to five minutes."

The key is specificity about when, what tool, and what surprised you. Use real timestamps ("this morning," "last Tuesday") and real tools from your stack. The reader trusts specific details more than general claims.

**Anti-examples (what to avoid):**

1. "I'm going to walk you through my AI workflow step by step" (Announces what it will do instead of doing it.)
2. "Let me show you something cool I built" (Vague promise with no specific detail. The reader has to trust you before they have any reason to.)

## Hook Type 5: The Question That Reframes

Take a question everyone is asking and offer a better one. The reader stays because you shifted their frame and they want to see where the new frame leads.

**Structure:** Everyone is asking [popular question]. Better question: [your reframe that reveals a hidden assumption].

**Examples:**

1. "Everyone is asking which AI model is best. Better question: what does your AI actually know about your business? Because the answer to that determines output quality more than the model ever will."
2. "Everyone is asking how to write better prompts. Better question: why are you starting from a blank prompt every time instead of giving the model permanent context about how you work?"
3. "Everyone is asking whether AI will replace developers. Better question: what happens to your team when code stops being the slow part and review, context, and decision making become the bottleneck?"

The reframe should come from your [[philosophy]] and reveal a hidden assumption your [[audience/]] holds. The best reframes shift the reader's attention from the obvious variable to the one that actually matters.

**Anti-examples (what to avoid):**

1. "Have you ever wondered why your AI outputs suck?" (Aggressive question that insults the reader before offering anything.)
2. "What if I told you everything you know about AI is wrong?" (Overused reveal framing. Rarely delivers on the promise.)

## Hooks to Avoid

These patterns appear on dozens of content accounts. Using them signals that the post will be generic, regardless of the actual content.

| Pattern | Why it fails |
|---------|-------------|
| "The only guide you need" | Overpromises and reads like SEO bait. Nobody believes it. |
| "If you haven't started X, you're falling behind" | Fear based urgency that treats the reader as behind. Builds resentment, not trust. |
| "F*ck [tool]" | Edgelord energy. Attracts engagement from people who want drama, not from people who want to learn. |
| "This changes everything" | Said about every minor update. The phrase has lost all meaning. |
| "I can't believe more people aren't talking about this" | Implies you have secret knowledge. The reader can smell the setup. |
| "Stop doing X. Start doing Y." | Command style opening that assumes authority you have not earned yet in the post. |
| Any opening that could appear on 50 other accounts in your niche | If you can swap in any other creator's name and the post still works, the hook is too generic. |

## Platform-Specific Hook Principles

Different platforms and audiences respond to different hook styles. Refer to your [[config]] for which platforms you use and [[voice/brand-voice]] for tone guidelines on each.

**What stops the scroll on professional/business platforms:**

- A number in the first line (hours, currency, a percentage). Numbers are concrete in a sea of abstract posts.
- Something the reader recognizes from their own week but has never seen articulated clearly.
- A short story that starts in the middle. Drop the reader into a meeting, a conversation, a moment. Do not set the scene first.
- A claim that goes against what everyone is currently saying, backed by something specific.

**What gets skipped on professional/business platforms:**

- "I keep seeing the same pattern at companies" and any variation. This opener signals a lecture, not a story.
- Opening with a question ("Have you ever wondered..."). The reader answers "no" internally and scrolls.
- Starting with context instead of the point. If the first two lines are setup, the reader will never reach the payoff.
- Anything that sounds like it was written for an audience instead of to one specific person.

Adapt these principles to your specific platform and language settings from [[config]]. If you post in multiple languages, write each version from scratch for that audience rather than translating. See [[engine/repurpose-rules]] for the full protocol.
